Squat Technique

The squat is the king of all lifts, targeting the glutes, quads, and core.

A proper squat requires bracing the core and maintaining a neutral spine.

Depth is key—hips should drop below the knees for competition standards.

Bar placement can be high-bar or low-bar depending on preference.

Shoes with a slight heel can improve stability and depth.

squat(weight, reps, sets) braceCore("inhale", "tight") adjustStance("shoulder-width") trackDepth("hip", "knee") formCheck("video")
  • Neutral spine
  • Braced core
  • Controlled descent
  • Explosive ascent
  • Proper footwear
Bench Press Fundamentals

The bench press builds upper body strength, focusing on the chest, triceps, and shoulders.

Grip width significantly impacts muscle activation.

A stable leg drive provides full-body tension and helps with pressing power.

Retract and depress the scapulae to protect the shoulders.

Keep wrists stacked over elbows for maximal force transfer.

benchPress(225, 5, 3) setGrip("medium") scapLock("retract", "depress") legDrive("engaged") wristAlignment("neutral")
  • Scapular retraction
  • Elbow angle
  • Bar path
  • Wrist stacking
  • Foot placement
Deadlift Mastery

The deadlift is a full-body compound lift with massive carryover to strength sports.

Choose between conventional and sumo based on biomechanics and comfort.

Engage the lats and brace before initiating the lift.

Keep the bar close to the shins and pull in a straight line.

Lockout by squeezing glutes, not hyperextending the back.

deadlift(315, 3, 5) selectStyle("sumo") latTension("tight") barPath("straight") gluteLockout("squeeze")
  • Flat back
  • Bar close to shins
  • Neutral neck
  • Hip hinge
  • Strong grip
Accessory Lifts

Accessory work reinforces weak points in your main lifts.

These include variations and isolation movements to target specific muscles.

Rotate accessories every few weeks to avoid plateaus.

Keep reps higher and focus on quality over quantity.

Record your sessions to monitor progress and form.

addAccessory("pause squat") focusMuscle("hamstrings") repRange(8, 12) tempo("3-1-1") trackIsolation("single-leg")
  • Bulgarian split squats
  • Glute bridges
  • Lat pulldowns
  • Incline dumbbell press
  • Hamstring curls
Warmups and Mobility

A good warmup primes the nervous system and increases range of motion.

Mobility drills should target joints and muscles involved in your session.

Foam rolling can release trigger points and improve blood flow.

Dynamic stretches are preferable pre-workout, static post-workout.

Warming up improves performance and reduces injury risk.

warmup("dynamic") targetJoint("hips") foamRoll("IT band") bandDrills("shoulder") activateGlutes()

  • Hip openers
  • Foam rolling quads
  • Scap pushups

Common splits include full body, upper/lower, and push/pull/legs.

Consistency and recovery should guide your choice.

Customize your volume and intensity throughout the week.

Training splits help manage fatigue and target muscle groups more efficiently.

A balance of protein, carbs, and fats is essential.

Pre- and post-workout nutrition optimizes energy and muscle repair.

Hydration is critical for muscle function and joint health.

Meal timing and frequency can vary based on training intensity.

Sleep and Recovery

Sleep is where strength gains and repair happen.

Aim for 7–9 hours of uninterrupted sleep each night.

Recovery days should be built into your training plan.

Sleep supports hormone regulation and CNS restoration.

Use tools like meditation or blue-light blockers to improve sleep quality.

sleepHours(8) useRecovery("active") scheduleRestDays(2) optimizeSleep("dark", "quiet") monitorHRV()

Periodization 101

Periodization is the structured progression of training over time.

It includes phases like hypertrophy, strength, and peaking.

Helps manage fatigue and avoid burnout.

Linear, undulating, and block periodization are popular models.

Deload weeks allow for adaptation and recovery.

setPhase("hypertrophy") deloadWeek() trackCycle("4 weeks") progressiveOverload() switchModel("block")

RPE and RIR

RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) and RIR (Reps in Reserve) help gauge intensity.

RPE is a scale from 1 to 10 used to measure effort.

RIR refers to how many reps you could’ve done before failure.

These tools build autoregulated programs that adapt to how you feel.

Helpful for avoiding overtraining and injury.

rateSet(8) recordRIR(2) adjustLoadByRPE() weeklyAverageRPE() logTraining("RIR", 1)
  • RPE 6 = 4 reps left
  • RPE 10 = max effort
  • Track daily
  • Adjust on bad days
  • Autoregulate

Cutting vs Bulking

Cutting aims to reduce body fat, bulking to increase lean mass.

Bulking requires a calorie surplus and heavy training.

Cutting needs a modest deficit and high protein intake.

Tracking your weight and measurements is key to either goal.

Avoid drastic calorie swings—consistency is better.

setGoal("bulk") trackIntake() adjustMacros() logWeightWeekly() bodyfatEstimate()
  • Surplus = bulk
  • Deficit = cut
  • High protein
  • Strength focus
  • Track weekly

Injury Prevention

Preventing injuries keeps your progress consistent and long-term.

Proper warmup and technique are your best defenses.

Listen to early warning signs like aches or tightness.

Rest and recovery are part of training, not an interruption.

Mobility work and prehab drills are invaluable.

prehab("shoulders") logPain("left knee") skipPRDay() mobilityRoutine() recoverMode("priority")

Respect for the gym space and others builds a better training environment.

Headphones in, attitude out.
